Title:
Adaptive Wavelet–Guided Bilateral Fusion: A Lightweight, Training-Free Image Denoising Method for Resource-Constrained Visual Sensing Systems

Abstract:
Image sensors deployed on embedded and Internet-of-Things (IoT) platforms routinely operate under tight latency and power budgets that preclude the use of deep convolutional denoisers, while the additive sensor noise they must remove still degrades downstream perception and analysis tasks. This paper presents the Adaptive Wavelet–Guided Bilateral (AWGB) filter, a training-free denoising method that fuses a wavelet-domain BayesShrink estimate with a self-guided, noise-adaptive bilateral filter through a spatially varying weight map derived from local gradient energy. Because the noise level driving every stage is estimated directly from the input via a robust median-absolute-deviation (MAD) estimator, the method requires no training data, no per-image hyperparameter retuning, and no GPU. We evaluate AWGB on 80 standard grayscale benchmark images (the full Set12 and BSD68 datasets) at three Gaussian noise levels, against five classical baselines that we implement and execute ourselves under matched conditions (Gaussian, median, bilateral, non-local means, wavelet-only thresholding), together with BM3D as a non-lightweight fidelity reference, and against literature-reported numbers for five representative deep and transformer-based denoisers (DnCNN, FFDNet, DRUNet, SwinIR, Restormer) for qualitative context. AWGB attains the best mean PSNR rank among all lightweight, training-free methods tested, improving on wavelet-only thresholding by a statistically significant margin (paired t-test p = 1.1×10⁻⁴⁷, Wilcoxon p = 6.8×10⁻³⁵, 92% win rate over 240 conditions) while running in 16–33 ms per image on a single CPU core — five to nine times faster than non-local means and roughly 480–740 times faster than BM3D. An ablation reported in this paper shows that an earlier design using a wavelet-guided joint bilateral filter, rather than the self-guided filter used in the final method, actually underperformed wavelet-only thresholding in 70% of conditions, because its output was too correlated with the wavelet estimate to contribute independent information under fusion; we report this negative result alongside the positive one because it materially shaped the final design. AWGB does not surpass BM3D, DnCNN, FFDNet, DRUNet, SwinIR, or Restormer in raw reconstruction fidelity, and we do not claim otherwise; these deep and transformer-based methods exceed BM3D's BSD68 PSNR by 0.6–1.4 dB but require GPU inference and tens of thousands to tens of millions of trained parameters. AWGB is intended as a practical denoising stage for visual sensing pipelines where that infrastructure is unavailable, not as a replacement for it.

Keywords:
Image denoising, wavelet thresholding, bilateral filtering, adaptive fusion, lightweight image processing, visual sensing, edge computing, BSD68, Set12
